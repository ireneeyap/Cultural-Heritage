Tangible Cultural Heritage

refers to a physical or materialistic aspect of a culture that is significant to the local history and culture (Jokilehto, J. 2005).

Some of the examples of tangible cultural heritage are buildings, artifacts, artworks, physical places, and archeological sites that are passed down generation to generation (UNESCO, 2003). These are physical objects that represent a nation’s, tradition's, community’s historical representation.

Examples: Calle Crisologo, Rice Terraces, and Tabon Cave

Calle Crisologo in Vigan, Ilocus Sur

Calle Crisologo in Vigan, once known as Calle Escolta de Vigan during the 15th century, is a 500-meter heritage street lined with Spanish-era stone houses and cobblestone roads.

preserving colonial history, promoting cultural identity, and contributing to tourism and local economy (UNESCO n.d.; Hike to Mountain 2023).

Rice Terraces of the Philippine Cordilleras in Banaue, Ifugao

The Rice Terraces of the Philippine Cordilleras, created by the Ifugao over 2,000 years ago, are gravity-fed farming systems carved into mountain slopes.

This showcases showcasing remarkable indigenous engineering, sustainability, and deep cultural significance—recognized globally as one of the oldest and most unique rice terraces (UNESCO, n.d.)

Tabon Cave Complex in Lipuun Point, Palawan

It is a key cultural heritage site where the 16,500-year-old remains of the Tabon Man and other prehistoric artifacts offer vital insights into early Filipino life and culture.

The Tabon Cave Complex in Palawan, known as the "Cradle of Philippine Civilization" (UNESCO 2006; NCAA n.d.).

Intangible Cultural Heritage

refers to the non-physical aspects of culture that a community or a group of people recognize as a form of their cultural identity such as practices and rituals.

Unlike tangible heritage, which includes physical representation like objects or places, intangible heritages are preserved through the form of practices, rituals and cultural influences. Protecting intangible cultural heritage contributes to the preservation of the vibrant traditions that enrich people's lives by connecting with their ancient ancestors or their culture (Kurin, R. 2004).

Aklan Piña Handloom

Aklan piña handloom is a traditional textile from Aklan, made from hand-extracted pineapple leaf fibers of the native "piña Bisaya," woven by artisans into clothing and cultural items, and recognized by UNESCO as an intangible cultural heritage (UNESCO, 2023).

Hudhud Chants of the Ifugao

The Hudhud chants are ancient narrative epics of the Ifugao people, performed during rice sowing, harvest, funerals, and rituals; passed down orally since the 7th century, they reflect ancestral values and traditions but are now endangered due to modern influences and loss of interest among the youth (UNESCO, 2008).
